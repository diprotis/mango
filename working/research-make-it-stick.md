# Research Report: Learning Science behind "Make It Stick"

> **Purpose.** Grounds the `make-it-stick` catalog entry (`backend/src/shared/catalog_data.py`)
> in verified cognitive-science research. This is an original synthesis of the
> *findings and primary literature*; it does **not** reproduce the book's text.
> Produced by the deep-research workflow (5 search angles → 23 sources → 109 claims
> → 25 adversarially verified, 22 confirmed). Confidence is per-claim below.

## Central thesis (high confidence)

*Make It Stick* (Brown, Roediger & McDaniel, 2014) argues humans have a vast
capacity to learn, but the most effective strategies are **counterintuitive and
effortful**, while popular ones (rereading, highlighting, cramming) feel
productive yet fail. The organizing principle, from Robert & Elizabeth Bjork, is a
**learning-vs-performance dissociation**: conditions that make performance improve
fast *during training* often fail to produce durable learning, whereas "desirable
difficulties" that slow apparent progress optimize long-term retention and transfer.

## Verified principles

1. **Retrieval practice / testing effect** (high). Recalling from memory beats
   rereading for durable retention. Roediger & Karpicke (2006, *Psychological
   Science* 17(3):249–255): after 1 week, tested group recalled **56% vs 42%**
   restudy (Exp 1); **61% vs 40%** despite reading the passage 3.4× vs 14.2× (Exp 2).
   Foundational modern work: Roediger & Karpicke; phenomenon dates to Abbott (1909).
2. **Crossover by delay** (high) — corrects the "cramming works" intuition.
   *Immediately*, restudy can win (81% vs 75%); on a **delayed** test, testing wins.
   The retrieval benefit emerges over time. Cite as "can be more effective," not always.
3. **Transfer** (high). Testing transfers to new/inferential questions better than
   restudy (Butler 2010), but the effect is **moderate and conditional** (meta-analytic
   d≈0.40, Pan & Rickard 2018) — not universal.
4. **Retrieval > elaborative concept mapping** (high, 2-0 vote). Karpicke & Blunt
   (2011, *Science* 331:772–775). Modern nuance: best to *combine* mapping with retrieval.
5. **Spaced practice** (high). Distributed study beats massed/cramming for long-term
   memory (Ebbinghaus 1885; Cepeda et al. 2006 meta-analysis — cite generally, the
   exact "259/271" figure was **refuted**; Bahrick et al. 1993: recall highest at
   56-day spacing; Dunlosky et al. 2013 rate it HIGH utility).
6. **Interleaving** (high). Mixing problem types beats blocked practice (Taylor &
   Rohrer 2010: 77% vs 38%, d=1.21). Requires familiarity with the categories mixed.
7. **Generation effect** (high). Slamecka & Graf (1978, *JEP:HLM* 4(6):592–604):
   self-generated answers are better remembered than the same words read.
8. **Desirable difficulties** (high). Term coined by Robert Bjork (1994). Difficulties
   help **only** if the learner has the prior knowledge to meet them, else they become
   "undesirable." Mechanism: New Theory of Disuse (Bjork & Bjork 1992) — storage vs
   retrieval strength; forgetting can enhance learning.
9. **Fluency illusion / illusion of competence** (high). Rereading produces
   familiarity/perceptual fluency misread as mastery (Bjork & Bjork 2011; Dunlosky et
   al. 2013 rate rereading & highlighting LOW utility).

## Boundary conditions (state honestly; do not omit)

- Testing-effect advantage emerges over **delay**; immediately, rereading can win.
- Transfer from testing is real but **moderate/conditional**.
- Desirable difficulties require **adequate prior knowledge**.
- Interleaving requires **familiarity** with the categories being mixed.
